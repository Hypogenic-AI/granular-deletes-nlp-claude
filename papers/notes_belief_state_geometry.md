# Notes: Transformers Represent Belief State Geometry in their Residual Stream

**Citation:** Shai, A. S., Marzen, S. E., Teixeira, L., Gietelink Oldenziel, A., & Riechers, P. M. (2024). Transformers Represent Belief State Geometry in their Residual Stream. *NeurIPS 2024*. arXiv:2405.15943v3.

---

## 1. Key Contribution: How Belief State Geometry Appears in the Residual Stream

The central claim is that transformers trained on next-token prediction do not merely learn to mimic the surface statistics of their training data — they learn the **meta-dynamics of belief updating** over the hidden states of the data-generating process.

More precisely, the paper argues and empirically demonstrates that:

- Transformers internally represent **belief states**: probability distributions over the hidden states of the underlying generative process, conditioned on the observed token sequence so far.
- These belief states are **linearly embedded** in the residual stream — there exists an affine map from residual stream activations to the probability simplex over hidden states such that the geometric arrangement of belief states is faithfully recovered.
- This geometry can be **highly nontrivial**, including fractal structures, and the transformer nonetheless represents it accurately.
- The representation goes **beyond next-token prediction**: transformers maintain distinctions between belief states that have identical next-token distributions but differ in their full future distributions. This happens because discarding such distinctions would compromise future next-token accuracy later in the sequence.

The theoretical grounding comes from **computational mechanics**, specifically the observation (Shalizi & Crutchfield, 2001) that optimal prediction requires distinguishing all pasts that induce distinct probability distributions over the entire future — the so-called causal states. Transformers appear to learn exactly this structure.

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1 Data Generating Processes: Hidden Markov Models

Training data is generated by **edge-emitting hidden Markov models (HMMs)**. An HMM has:
- Hidden states `s ∈ S`
- Tokens (observations) `x ∈ X`
- Token-labeled transition matrices `{T^(x)}`, where `T^(x)_{i,j} = Pr(x, s_j | s_i)` — the joint probability of emitting token `x` and transitioning to state `s_j` from state `s_i`.

Two specific HMMs are used as experimental subjects:

**Mess3 Process:** A 3-state HMM with token vocabulary `{A, B, C}`. The transition matrices are given explicitly (see Appendix A.3). This process has a fractal mixed-state presentation (MSP), providing a non-trivial test case.

**Random-Random-XOR (RRXOR) Process:** A 5-state HMM with binary tokens `{0, 1}`. Its MSP has 36 distinct belief states. Critically, many of these belief states are **degenerate** in terms of next-token predictions — multiple distinct belief states yield the same optimal next-token distribution. This process has zero pairwise correlation but rich higher-order structure.

### 2.2 The Mixed-State Presentation (MSP)

The **mixed-state presentation** (from computational mechanics) is the formal structure describing how an optimal observer updates beliefs about hidden states as tokens are observed.

Given current belief state `η` (a probability distribution over hidden states, represented as a row vector) and a newly observed token `x`, the belief update rule is:

```
η' = η T^(x) / (η T^(x) 1)
```

where `1` is a column vector of ones (the denominator normalizes the result). Starting from the stationary distribution `π` (the initial belief state for stationary ergodic processes), this update rule traces out a trajectory in the probability simplex as tokens are observed.

The set of all reachable belief states, together with the transition probabilities between them, constitutes the MSP. These belief states live naturally in a probability simplex of dimension `|S| - 1` (where `|S|` is the number of hidden states). The geometric arrangement of these points in the simplex is called the **belief state geometry**.

Key property: each belief state encodes a full distribution over *all possible futures*, not just the next token. Belief states with identical next-token distributions can still be distinct points in the simplex if their long-range future distributions differ.

### 2.3 Finding Belief States in the Residual Stream: Linear Probing

The procedure for checking whether belief state geometry is linearly represented:

1. **Train a transformer** on sequences generated by a ground-truth HMM.
2. **Record residual stream activations** `a ∈ R^{d_resid}` at all context window positions for all input sequences (either a single layer or a concatenation of all layers).
3. **Compute ground-truth belief states** `b ∈ R^{|S|}` for each input sequence using the HMM transition matrices and the MSP update rule.
4. **Fit a linear regression** (affine map): minimize `Σ_i ||b_i - (W a_i + c)||^2` over weight matrix `W ∈ R^{|S| × d_resid}` and bias `c ∈ R^{|S|}`. This finds the linear subspace of activation space that best maps to the probability simplex.
5. **Visualize and evaluate** the projected geometry against the ground-truth MSP geometry.

Controls run to rule out artifacts:
- Analysis at multiple training checkpoints (geometry emerges gradually, ruling out initialization artifacts).
- 20%/80% cross-validation (held-out data shows similar geometry and MSE).
- Shuffle control: belief state labels are shuffled while preserving the overall fractal shape; the regression collapses all points to the simplex center, confirming that the structural match is not an artifact of projecting high-dimensional data.
- Loss vs. MSE correlation: lower cross-entropy loss correlates with lower MSE in the belief-state regression (Figure S2), establishing a functional link.

## 3. How They Map Residual Stream Geometry

The mapping procedure is as follows:

- Activations live in `R^{d_resid}` (64 dimensions in experiments). Belief states live in a `(|S|-1)`-dimensional simplex.
- A weight matrix `W ∈ R^{|S| × d_resid}` is learned via linear regression to project activations into the simplex.
- For the Mess3 process (3 hidden states), the projection identifies a **2D subspace** of the 64-dimensional residual stream.
- The geometry of this projected 2D subspace is then compared visually and quantitatively (via MSE and pairwise distance correlation) to the theoretical MSP geometry.

For the **layer-wise analysis** (RRXOR case):
- The regression is applied to activations from each individual layer separately, as well as to a **concatenation of activations across all layers**.
- Mean squared error is reported per layer and for the concatenation, revealing where in the network belief state information lives.

Pairwise distance comparisons are used to distinguish whether the discovered geometry is explained by next-token predictions or requires full belief states. For the RRXOR process: correlation with ground-truth belief state distances is R² = 0.95, while correlation with next-token prediction distances is only R² = 0.31.

## 4. Key Findings About Geometric Structure

### Fractal Structure
- The Mess3 MSP has an intricate **fractal geometry** in the probability simplex. The transformer's residual stream linearly represents this fractal structure, even though the model was never explicitly shown hidden states or informed of the HMM structure.
- The fractal arises because Bayesian updating on non-deterministic HMMs applies the same belief-update map repeatedly, folding the simplex into itself, producing self-similar structure.

### Belief State Geometry Is Linearly Represented
- For the Mess3 process, a linear projection of the **final layer** residual stream (64-dimensional) cleanly recovers the 2D fractal belief state geometry.
- Results hold whether activations are taken before or after the final LayerNorm (marginally lower MSE after LayerNorm: 0.0003 vs. 0.0004).

### Geometry Can Be Distributed Across Layers
- For the RRXOR process, the belief state geometry is **not** found in any individual layer (including the final layer).
- However, it is fully recoverable in the **concatenation of residual stream activations across all layers**.
- This is theoretically expected: because RRXOR belief states are degenerate in their next-token distributions, the model has no incentive to maintain those distinctions at the final layer (which feeds directly into the unembedding). The distinctions are preserved in intermediate layers but collapsed before the output.
- Mess3 belief state geometry, by contrast, **persists to the final layer** because all Mess3 belief state distinctions are necessary for the next-token prediction.

### Information Beyond Next-Token Prediction
- Multiple distinct belief states can share identical next-token distributions (especially in RRXOR). The transformer nevertheless maintains internal distinctions between such states.
- The reason: discarding these distinctions would impair the model's ability to produce correct next-token predictions at *future* context positions. To minimise long-run loss, the model must track the entire belief state, not just the marginal next-token distribution.
- This is formalized by the result (Shalizi & Crutchfield, 2001) that optimal prediction requires retaining all information distinguishing pasts that lead to distinct full-future distributions.

### Linearity Is a Non-Trivial Empirical Finding
- It is possible in principle to solve next-token prediction without linearly representing belief states. A handcrafted transformer (with weights set by a human) was shown to solve the RRXOR task without a linear belief state representation (Goldstein, 2024, cited as Ref. [11]).
- That SGD-trained transformers find linear representations is therefore a genuine empirical discovery, not a mathematical necessity.

### Emergence Over Training
- The fractal belief state geometry emerges **gradually** over the course of training, not at initialization. This rules out architectural artifacts.
- Lower cross-entropy loss correlates with lower MSE in the belief-state linear regression across training checkpoints.

## 5. Models and Datasets Used

### Transformer Architecture (Experiments)
- **Context window:** 10 tokens
- **Model dimension (d_resid):** 64
- **Layers:** 4
- **Attention heads:** 1 per layer, head dimension 8
- **MLP dimension:** 256
- **Activation function:** ReLU
- **Attention:** Causal masking
- **Normalization:** Layer normalization
- **Optimizer:** SGD, learning rate 0.01, batch size 64, no weight decay
- **Training duration:** 1,000,000 epochs
- **Library:** TransformerLens (Nanda & Bloom, 2022)

### Datasets
- **Mess3 Process:** Sequences over vocabulary `{A, B, C}`, generated from a 3-state HMM. The MSP has fractal structure in a 2-simplex.
- **RRXOR Process:** Binary sequences over `{0, 1}`, generated from a 5-state HMM. The MSP has 36 belief states in a 4-simplex, with many next-token-degenerate states.

Both processes have **infinite Markov order** — no finite-order Markov approximation captures them exactly — providing meaningful complexity despite the small number of hidden states.

### Scale Limitation Acknowledged
- All experiments use HMMs with 3–5 states and vocabularies of 2–3 tokens — far simpler than production language models (>50,000 token vocabularies, billions of parameters).
- For realistic natural language, the belief state simplex would have far higher dimension than the residual stream, implying **compressed representations** if the framework applies. Understanding what is preserved vs. discarded in this compression is identified as a key open problem.

## 6. Connection to Logit Lens and Interpretability

### Logit Lens Connection
The paper connects to the **logit lens** / "Future Lens" line of work (Pal et al., 2023, Ref. [25]):
- The Future Lens framework shows that individual hidden states in transformers trained on natural language contain information about **multiple future tokens**, not just the next one.
- This is consistent with the belief state interpretation: belief states by definition encode distributions over the entire future, so if transformers represent belief states, their hidden states should predict far-future tokens.
- The paper cites [25]'s finding that "the predictive accuracy of the residual stream with respect to far-future tokens peaks somewhere in intermediate layers" as consistent with their framework — belief state information is distributed across layers.

### Interpretability Implications
- The MSP provides a **ground-truth, theory-derived notion of what structure should be internally represented**, in contrast to much interpretability work where ground truth is unknown.
- The belief state geometry is **model-agnostic**: the same geometry is predicted regardless of architecture, as long as the model (i) uses a residual stream and (ii) is pretrained on future-token prediction. Recent follow-up work (Pepper, 2024, Ref. [26]) confirmed that RNNs also represent belief state geometry in their hidden states.
- The framework naturally explains why **board game transformers** (e.g., OthelloGPT, Refs. [17, 23]) develop internal board state representations: in a deterministic sequential game, each board state uniquely determines future move distributions, so board states map one-to-one onto belief states.
- The framework suggests that known geometric patterns in LLM internals — e.g., days of the week lying on a circle (Engels et al., 2024, Ref. [9]) — may be explainable via the MSP structure of how those concepts are distributed in training data.

### Features vs. Belief States
- The paper explicitly addresses the relationship between belief states and the "features" studied in mechanistic interpretability.
- The mapping is not one-to-one: a single belief state may encompass multiple features, and a single feature may appear across multiple belief states.
- The MSP provides a principled ground truth for what the model is computing, but the exact correspondence to the features studied in sparse autoencoder / dictionary learning work remains an open problem.

## 7. Implications for Understanding What Information Is Stored Where

### Layer-Wise Distribution of Belief Information
- Whether belief states are found at the final layer or distributed across all layers depends on the **relationship between belief state structure and next-token predictions**:
  - If all belief state distinctions map to distinct next-token distributions (Mess3): belief state geometry persists to the final layer, because the unembedding needs it.
  - If some belief state distinctions are degenerate in next-token space (RRXOR): the model may collapse those distinctions before the unembedding. The belief state geometry is spread across intermediate layers but absent at the final layer.

### Residual Stream Dimensionality
- Any model achieving minimal loss must have **at least as many residual stream dimensions as there are states in the minimal generative model** (the epsilon-machine) of the training distribution. The belief-state simplex gives a principled lower bound on required model capacity.

### Information Beyond the Next Token
- The residual stream at each position encodes, in a distributed way across layers, the **full belief state** — a sufficient statistic for predicting the entire future, not just the next token.
- This means the residual stream is storing substantially more than a next-token logit distribution. It stores the model's current probabilistic "location" in the latent space of the data-generating process.

### Synchronization to Context
- As context grows, the model's belief state becomes more concentrated (less uncertain) about the hidden state of the world. The framework predicts **rates of in-context convergence** as a function of the MSP structure.
- Different users in the training data can be modelled as disconnected ergodic components of a non-ergodic process; the model must synchronize both to the user type (ergodic component) and to the user's current state.

### Bayesian Updating as a Circuit
- The representation evidence is consistent with the transformer implementing something like **Bayesian belief updating** (as described by the MSP update rule) as a circuit, but this was not directly tested. The paper establishes that belief states are represented linearly; whether the transformer implements the MSP dynamics mechanistically is left for future work.

### Non-Stationary and Natural Language Extension
- Even in non-stationary or non-ergodic settings (natural language), the authors expect fractal-like self-similar structure in residual stream activations, arising from the same Bayesian updating logic applied to non-deterministic computational structure.
- Any token sequence dataset can be represented as generated by an HMM (allowing infinite states and non-ergodicity), so the framework is in principle general. However, direct empirical validation on natural language remains future work.
